# Esteban Cuenca

Esteban Cuenca is an Argentine producer based in Amsterdam. He develops and produces fiction, documentary, and immersive work at the intersection of image, sound, and memory.

His feature documentary *MAILIN* (dir. Maria Silvia Esteve), supported by the Bertha Fund and CNC, premiered in the International Competition at IDFA 2025 and has screened at over twelve festivals. He is producing *Phantom Data* (Cannes Immersive Market 2026) and developing the fiction feature *Fauces* (dir. Maria Silvia Esteve), previously supported by TorinoFilmLab and CNC. He also co-produced the animated short *It Was Raining* (dir. Chaky Lillini), 1st Prize at the Florida Animation Festival 2022.

Alongside his independent work, he operates as an integrated producer for global brands including Nike, Apple, Google, Adidas, Spotify, Heineken, and Philips, managing campaign production across EMEA, the US, and JAPAC.

## {eave} Workshop Motivation Statement — Inclusive Production Practices

I produce across borders as a matter of course, as part of Hana Films, a collective built around shared practice and co-authored work. My projects are multilingual, multi-territorial, and structurally dependent on co-production, which means these questions aren't abstract for me. I encounter them in development meetings, in contract negotiations, in casting decisions, and in the gaps between what frameworks promise and what they actually deliver.

The relationship between capital and rights is where the most persistent friction lives. Co-production agreements are designed to enable collaboration, but they rarely neutralise the gravitational pull of financing: the partner with the larger stake tends to accumulate creative decision-making power, regardless of whose story is being told or whose community is most affected. Capital and rights are supposed to be separate tracks. In practice they blur, and that blur shows up most visibly in contracts, in who holds approval rights, in who controls delivery, in whose name comes first. Producers without access to large funding markets find themselves negotiating their creative position as much as their budget, often without the tools or precedents to do it fairly.

I work between Europe and Latin America, which means I work between two systems that don't fully recognise each other. That gap isn't only financial, though the currency asymmetry alone shapes who can afford to wait through a long development, who has the institutional weight to hold their ground when a negotiation gets hard. I've been on both sides of that.

Requirements for creative participation per country are well-intentioned but difficult to honour with integrity. Real inclusion needs structural conditions, earlier partnerships, longer development phases, funding models that don't penalise slower and more deliberate collaboration. Without those, participation risks becoming a box-ticking exercise rather than real collaboration. The paperwork looks right. The process underneath it often doesn't.

Language is the challenge that never quite makes it into the budget. Working across multiple territories rarely means sharing a working language, and the costs of translation, across legal documents, creative development, and audience accessibility, tend to be absorbed quietly rather than planned for. Accessibility for D/deaf and hard-of-hearing collaborators and audiences belongs in the same conversation. Both tend to be treated as delivery problems rather than design decisions, and both add friction at exactly the moments when collaboration should be opening up rather than closing down.

Bringing people together matters in creative work, and I'm not interested in arguing against it. The question is whether the conditions around that — who travels to whom, who bears the cost, who gets excluded before the conversation even starts — reflect the values the production claims to hold. Inclusion isn't just about who is in the room. It's about whether the structure around the room makes genuine participation possible.

I'm applying to this workshop because I want practical tools, not just frameworks, to build productions where shared ownership and fair practice are designed in from the start, not added at the end. **I come from a specific position, Latin American by background, European by practice, collective by structure, and I know that position gives me a particular view of how these systems work and who they work for.** I want to sit with others who are navigating the same questions from different entry points, and I want to leave with something I can actually use.

**About HANA FILMS**

Based in Buenos Aires, Argentina, HANA Films is an audiovisual collective focused on the creation of auteur pieces. Founded by María Silvia Esteve in 2018, the collective has produced films that premiered in Cannes, won Locarno, and were shown at the MoMA, international galleries, and at more than 300 film festivals, including IDFA, HKIFF, Sitges, and MIFF.

HANA works across different mediums, including the video installations *Existe en el Reflejo*, *Espejismos Improbables*, *M, las preguntas*, and *CORTEX*, exhibited at La MEP — Paris 2024/2025.
